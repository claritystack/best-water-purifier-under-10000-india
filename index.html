RO vs UV vs UF — Which Do You Need?

Before choosing the best water purifier under 10000, understanding the three core technologies will save you from buying the wrong one entirely.

RO (Reverse Osmosis) forces water through a semi-permeable membrane that blocks dissolved salts, heavy metals, and most contaminants. It is the only technology that reduces TDS — which means it is non-negotiable if your water source is borewell, tanker, or any supply with TDS above 200 ppm. The trade-off is water wastage: standard RO systems discard 2 to 3 litres for every litre of clean water produced. Newer models with water-saving technology bring this closer to 1:1.

UV (Ultraviolet) uses a UV lamp to kill bacteria, viruses, and microorganisms but does not reduce TDS at all. It is effective for municipal water with low TDS but will not help with hard water or dissolved salts. UV purifiers are cheaper to run and waste no water, but they are the wrong choice if your TDS is above 200 ppm.

UF (Ultrafiltration) uses a hollow-fibre membrane to remove bacteria and some larger particles without electricity. It does not reduce TDS and does not kill viruses. It works best as an additional stage alongside RO or UV, not as a standalone purification method for Indian water conditions.

For most Indian households — especially those relying on borewell, tanker, or mixed supply — an RO+UV combination is the minimum requirement. All five purifiers on this list use RO as their primary technology, which is the right call for the Indian market.

What to Look For in a Water Purifier Under ₹10,000?

Choosing the best water purifier under 10000 comes down to matching the technology to your actual water source — not just picking the one with the most stages or the lowest price.

Know your TDS before buying. TDS testers cost ₹200 to ₹500 on Amazon and take 10 seconds to use. If your water TDS is below 200 ppm, a UV purifier may be sufficient. If it is between 200 and 500 ppm, RO is recommended. Above 500 ppm, RO is non-negotiable. Most Indian households on borewell or tanker water will be in the 300 to 1500 ppm range — which means RO is the right choice for the vast majority of buyers looking at the best water purifier under 10000.

Storage capacity should match family size. A 6 to 7 litre tank is adequate for a family of 3 to 4 people for drinking and cooking. Families of 5 or more, or households that use purified water for cooking in large quantities, should look at 8 litres or above. The Aqua D Pure at 12 litres offers the most storage on this list at the lowest price.

Water wastage is a real cost. Standard RO systems waste 2 to 3 litres per litre produced. In a family that consumes 10 litres of purified water daily, that is 20 to 30 litres of wasted water every day — over 700 litres a month. Models with water-saving technology like the Pureit Wave Plus and Aquaguard Aquasaver significantly reduce this. In water-scarce cities, this is not a minor consideration.

After-sales service is the most underrated factor. An RO purifier requires filter replacement every 6 to 12 months and periodic servicing. Brands with strong service networks — Aquaguard, Kent, Pureit — make this process straightforward. Newer or lesser-known brands may offer better specs on paper but leave you struggling to find a technician or replacement filters in your city.

Annual maintenance cost matters more than purchase price. Budget ₹2,000 to ₹4,000 per year for filter replacement and AMC regardless of which purifier you buy. A purifier that costs ₹7,000 with ₹4,000 annual AMC is more expensive over three years than one that costs ₹9,500 with a ₹2,000 service plan included.
